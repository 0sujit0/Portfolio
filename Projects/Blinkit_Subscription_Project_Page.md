## 7. What We Heard — Five Findings That Shaped the Product

**Section treatment:** Parchment.

**Eyebrow:** `USER RESEARCH · PRIMARY`
**H2:** What we heard
**Subhead** (Anthropic Sans 17px `Olive Gray`): Five patterns surfaced consistently across the 42-response survey and 10 depth interviews. Each mapped directly to a pain cluster in Section 9.

**Numbered finding list** (each row: large serif number `01–05` in `Terracotta #c96442` at 32px weight 500 on the left, title in Anthropic Serif 20.8px weight 500 on the right, body below in Anthropic Sans 16px `Olive Gray` line-height 1.60. Horizontal divider between rows in Border Cream `#f0eee6`.):

- **01 — High frequency, low loyalty.** ~70% buy essentials 2+ times a week but switch freely between Blinkit, Zepto, Instamart, and the kirana next door. Price and mood drive each order.
- **02 — Flexibility > discount.** The top hesitation isn't price — it's commitment. Users said *"I want to order only when needed"* more often than *"it's too expensive."*
- **03 — Trust collapses on perishables.** Wilted greens, broken eggs, and near-expiry milk are category-killers for recurring orders. One bad delivery kills a month of subscription.
- **04 — MOV kills single-SKU intent.** The ₹199 MOV forces users to add fillers for a ₹30 milk run. They abandon the cart or go to the kirana. Subscription must solve this structurally.
- **05 — Known awareness, near-zero adoption.** Users have heard of milk baskets and Amazon Subscribe & Save. They haven't adopted because current models feel rigid, prepaid, or both.

### P1 — Commitment Anxiety
*Flexibility + refund + trust in pause*
Users will not subscribe if they fear being charged for something they don't want or can't stop. This is the dominant blocker across the sample.

### P2 — Wastage Risk
*Fixed cadence vs. variable consumption*
Household usage flexes with guests, travel, and leftovers. Rigid weekly delivery creates spoilage and guilt.

### P3 — Economic Friction
*MOV, delivery fee, kirana parity*
A ₹30 milk order + ₹199 MOV + ₹25 delivery fee makes Blinkit 2× kirana. Subscription must remove this structurally, not paper over it with discounts.

### P4 — Cognitive Load
*Forgetting + manual reorder drudgery*
Keeping staples stocked is a background tax. Users want the app to carry this load — but not silently, not without their explicit consent each cycle.

## 11. Solution Framework — Three Tiers

**Section treatment:** Parchment.

**Eyebrow:** `SOLUTIONS`
**H2:** Three tiers. Build in order. Trust first, growth last.
**Subhead** (Anthropic Sans 17px `Olive Gray`):
Nine solutions, tiered by what they unlock. Tier 1 unblocks adoption. Tier 2 unlocks perishables and trust. Tier 3 lifts LTV and adoption depth. The tiering is the build order — not a menu to be re-sequenced.

### 11.1 Tier 1 — Foundation *(Unblocks adoption)*

**Three Ivory cards in a row, 16px radius, padding 24px, 1px Border Cream.** Above the row: a Terracotta pill label `TIER 1 · FOUNDATION` in Anthropic Sans 12px uppercase, letter-spacing 0.12px.

- **01 — Pause / Skip / Cancel in one tap.** The foundational trust move. A user must be able to stop the subscription with a single gesture, no dialog, no retention modal. *Solves P1.*
- **02 — Pay-per-delivery (no prepaid lock-in).** Reverses the default industry contract. Users pay only for what lands at the door. *Solves P1.*
- **03 — Instant refund on any failure.** If an item is missing, spoiled, or damaged, the refund is credited to the user wallet before the rider leaves. *Solves P1.*

### 11.2 Tier 2 — Differentiation *(Unlocks perishables & trust)*

Same card treatment. Label pill: `TIER 2 · DIFFERENTIATION` in Coral Accent (`#d97757`) bg, Ivory text.

- **01 — Freshness & damage guarantee.** Explicit guarantee on perishables — no fine print. Every eligible SKU carries the promise. *Solves P2.*
- **02 — Price-lock on subscribed SKUs (30 days).** Subscribers see the same price for 30 days on subscribed SKUs. Removes pricing anxiety. *Solves P3.*
- **03 — Weekly MOV (not per-order MOV).** Users meet a ₹450/week basket threshold instead of ₹199 per order. A ₹30 milk run now works economically. *Solves P3.*

### 11.3 Tier 3 — Growth *(Lifts LTV & adoption depth)*

Same card treatment. Label pill: `TIER 3 · GROWTH` in Warm Sand bg, Charcoal Warm text.

- **01 — Smart reminder → confirm to subscribe.** Gateway feature. A user reorders milk 6 times in 3 weeks → app suggests subscription with one tap. *Solves P4.*
- **02 — Flatmate / household shared subscriptions.** Multi-user billing, single delivery. Specifically for the PG Resident persona. *Solves P4.*
- **03 — Predictive quantity nudges.** *"You usually buy 2 loaves — want to switch to 3 before the weekend?"* Gentle automation without silent overreach. *Solves P2 + P4.*

**Design principle callout** (on Ivory, 24px radius, padding 32px, left-border `4px solid Terracotta`, centered):
*A user must be able to exit the subscription faster than they entered it.*

## 13. The Unit-Economics Truth

**Section treatment:** Switch to `Deep Dark #141413` — this is the thesis-defining commercial argument, deserves gravity.

**H2** (Serif 52px, Ivory): The Unit-Economics Truth

**Lead** (Anthropic Serif 20px line-height 1.60, Warm Silver, max-width 720px):
Subscription has to pay for itself — or it becomes a VC-subsidised growth hack that dies in a funding winter. The per-order economics of a single milk delivery are brutal. The case for building this feature lives entirely in what happens across twelve months of retention.

### 13.1 Per-Delivery Economics — 1L Milk Subscribed

**Table on Dark Surface `#30302e`, 16px radius, 1px `#30302e` border. Padding 24px. Table cells: Anthropic Sans 15px `Warm Silver`; numbers in Anthropic Mono 15px aligned right.**

*Indicative model · partner-margin and rider cost from published q-commerce disclosures*

| Line item | Amount |
|---|---|
| Selling price (1L milk) | **+₹62.00** |
| COGS (supplier) | −₹54.00 |
| Gross margin | +₹8.00 |
| Rider cost (batched × 5 orders) | −₹7.00 |
| Dark-store fulfillment cost | −₹2.50 |
| Payment + platform cost | −₹1.00 |
| **Contribution margin (per order)** | **−₹2.50** |
| LTV lift from retention (12 months) | +₹380.00 |

The per-order CM line gets a Terracotta left-border 4px and Coral Accent text. The LTV line gets a Terracotta highlight.

### 13.2 Three Truths About This Model

**Three dark cards side-by-side on desktop, stacked on mobile. Dark Surface `#30302e`, 16px radius, padding 28px.**

- **Per-order CM is negative.** Single-SKU daily orders lose money unless rider trips are batched across 4+ subscriptions per route. Batching is the core ops bet, not a footnote.
- **Retention is where this pays back.** A subscribed user retained 12 months generates ~₹380 of incremental GMV contribution. Payback: month 3–4. This isn't GMV arithmetic — it's LTV arithmetic.
- **Weekly MOV is the guardrail.** Remove the ₹199-per-order friction, but cap subscribed baskets at ₹450/week minimum. Protects CM while solving single-SKU pain. The MOV doesn't disappear — it moves upstream.

Stat number/header: Anthropic Serif 25px weight 500 `Coral Accent #d97757`. Description: Anthropic Sans 15px `Warm Silver`.

**Closer** (Anthropic Serif 32px weight 500 line-height 1.30, Ivory, max-width 720px):
This feature doesn't get built because it's profitable on day one. It gets built because it's the only defensible shape of profitability in month twelve.

## 17. Constraints & Assumptions

**Section treatment:** Parchment.

### 17.1 Constraints Considered

**Four Ivory cards** (16px radius, 1px Border Cream, padding 24px, 2-column grid on desktop). Each card has a typographic icon inside a small `Warm Sand` circle (32px) beside the heading.

**⚙ Technology**
- **Subscription ledger:** The pause/skip/refund UI is cheap; the ledger underneath is not. Must be transactional, auditable, and user-visible in real time.
- **MOV model re-architecture:** Weekly MOV requires a new basket-accumulation primitive. The existing per-order MOV logic cannot be reused.
- **Event instrumentation:** New events for subscription state changes (`sub_paused`, `sub_skipped`, `sub_cancelled`, `refund_credited`) don't exist yet.

**🏭 Operations**
- **Rider batching dependency:** Per-order CM is −₹2.50. Break-even requires ≥4 subscriptions per rider trip. Low-density pincodes must be gated.
- **Dark-store inventory reservation:** 110% of subscribed inventory must be reserved. Stockout on a subscribed delivery is a trust-killer, not a product glitch.
- **Cold-chain for perishables:** Phase 2 introduces dairy, eggs, curd — existing dark-store cold infrastructure must be audited before launch.

**🧠 User Behaviour**
- **Prepaid aversion:** 6 of 10 interviews surfaced this unprompted. Any pilot that introduces prepaid — even as an upsell — will contaminate learning.
- **Hostel / PG churn:** Persona 2 has seasonal address changes. One-tap cancel + full refund is not a feature; it's the entry condition for this cohort.
- **Flatmate friction:** Nobody wants to be the *"reorder person."* Shared subscriptions are a growth bet, not a trust bet — ship after trust is proven.

**💰 Financial**
- **Per-order CM is negative:** Every single-SKU daily delivery loses ₹2.50. Profitability lives in batching + 12-month LTV. Anyone evaluating on per-order margin will kill this product.
- **Weekly MOV ceiling:** Cap subscribed baskets at ₹450/week minimum. Below this, batching math breaks. Above this, user friction rises.
- **No marketing spend in Pilot:** Pilot must be opt-in from the existing user base. Paid acquisition introduces novelty bias and inflates D30 numbers.

### 17.2 Key Assumptions

**Table** (Ivory, 16px radius). The ★ next to A1 should be visually emphasized with a Terracotta star glyph — this is the most consequential assumption.

| # | Assumption | If True | If Wrong | How It's Tested |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **A1 ★** | **Batching ≥4 orders per rider trip achievable in high-density pincodes** (most consequential) | Unit economics close by Phase 3; national rollout viable | Per-order losses compound; product must be restructured or killed | Daily rider-trip audit from Day 1. Phase 3 gate is 4.0 orders/trip — failure triggers CFO review |
